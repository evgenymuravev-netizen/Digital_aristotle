01 — The problem

In cash-dominant trade, collections is the boss battle.

Saudi traditional trade runs on cash, trust and the rep's notebook. Merchants are thin-file to banks, distributors carry informal credit they can't price, and every lender who bolted credit onto someone else's transaction has had to chase repayment after the fact — the loss driver that kills books in this segment.

For the merchant

Invisible to banks, capped by cash

A shop turning SAR 50–500K a month has no formal credit history. Ramadan stock-ups are funded from the till, or not at all. The cost isn't interest — it's the empty shelf.

For the distributor

A credit desk run on intuition

Distributors already extend informal terms — underwritten by a rep's memory, collected on the route, written off quietly. An in-house financing arm takes 18–24 months and still doesn't solve collections.

For the lender

Detached credit dies chasing cash

Capiter blitz-scaled to 50,000 merchants and collapsed in 2022. Losses lag originations by 6–12 months; credit that doesn't own the repayment flow learns this the expensive way.

The structural answer

Don't chase the cash — own the rail it moves on. When repayment is a ledger event on a settlement rail you operate, collections stops being a department and becomes a property of the system.

02 — The product

One company, three chapters.

Own the flow. Out-underwrite everyone. Then sell the machine. Each chapter manufactures the asset the next one needs — which is why the order is the strategy.

03 — How it works

Two sides of one flow.

1

Onboard in one visit

The rep opens it on a normal route stop: Nafath identity, CR photo, PDPL consent and one-page Murabaha terms — in Arabic or Urdu. The limit goes live before the rep leaves.

2

Approve inside the order

Credit decided in <300ms at the rep's tablet or any checkout. Partial approvals rescue the basket: “SAR 9,000 on your line, SAR 3,400 cash.” Cost is a flat fee in riyals.

3

One date, met where cash moves

Obligations across all suppliers net into one weekly schedule — collected by sarie, auto-debit or rep cash-in, Ramadan-aware. No late fees; early settlement earns a rebate.

4

Repaying builds the asset

Every on-time cycle visibly grows the limit and writes to SIMAH — the merchant's first bureau-grade record, portable across every supplier on the rail.

04 — 2026 → 2031

It evolves in the only order that compounds.

Not a feature roadmap — a dependency chain. Each chapter manufactures the asset the next one needs, and nothing ships ahead of its evidence.

Own the flow→ Collections proof→ Proprietary data→ Engine edge→ Funded track record→ Rentable machine

2026 — Prove the rail in one city

What ships: settlement rail MVP with two anchor distributors; rep-assisted onboarding (Nafath + PDPL + Murabaha one-pager); financed orders with partial approvals; the repayment wallet (sarie / auto-debit / rep cash-in); a distributor console with AR & collections workflow; WhatsApp nudges.

Why first: collections is the binding constraint of the whole category — not demand, not data, not capital. Square lent $22B+ at <3% loss only because repayment rides its own rail; Capiter died chasing cash it didn't control. Until repayment capture is proven, every later product is a liability multiplier.

Exit criteria — unlocks Chapter 2
  • ≥60% of repayment captured on-rail
  • DPD better than the field baseline
  • Audited approval@loss vs an anchor's book
  • Institutional-grade loan tape from day one

2027–28 — Compound the engine

What ships: the merchant app (limits, one-tap repay, portable record); multi-distributor netting; the audited “+15pp at your loss” test; a marketplace checkout widget; the first bank warehouse facility; an engine-as-a-service pilot.

Why now, not before: the rail's Year-1 exhaust — settlement truth, repayment behaviour, ZATCA flows — is the feature set no off-rail lender can copy. The cheapest growth isn't more cities; it's approving more of the same flow at the same loss. A clean, seasoned tape makes banks fund the book, so capital stops being the ceiling.

Why the app arrives second: trust precedes surface. Merchants meet Tradepay through the rep; the app is the retention layer once they're transacting — not an acquisition bet against the channel.

Exit criteria — unlocks Chapter 3
  • Two years of seasoned vintages inside budget
  • Warehouse drawn; covenants green
  • Uplift verified on a book we don't own
  • Multi-distributor netting live

2029–31 — Open the machine

What ships: the Infrastructure Arm — a white-label console where a distributor launches its branded financing arm on Tradepay's stack in six weeks; Murabaha securitization of seasoned receivables; UAE then Egypt entry; aggregated, PDPL-clean trade intelligence for FMCG brands.

Why last — and why inevitable: in-house arms get built either way; the only question is on whose machine. By 2029 Tradepay holds what no in-house build can match — two years of audited collections, a measured approval edge, funded vintages. Tenants get decisions, rails and governance; never features, weights or pooled data — so every tenant widens the engine's lead instead of arming a rival.

Endgame metrics
  • %GMV settled on-rail — the position
  • approval@loss — the economics
  • Arm programs live — threats → tenants
  • Cost of funds falling as ABS lands
