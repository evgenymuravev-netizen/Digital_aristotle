Chapter 01
Kernel · Diagnosis

Name the real challenge.

A good strategy starts by honestly diagnosing the obstacle — not by listing ambitions. Tradepay is already live, with 700+ merchants and 95% on-time invoice settlement, so the obstacle is no longer proving merchants want the product. It is whether collection — today achieved by hustle at small scale — can be made structural enough to hold that 95% at 60× the merchants, profitably, before a better-capitalised rival gets there.

Who we serve

Micro and small traditional retail in Saudi Arabia: baqalas (40–100m²), mini-markets (100–500m²), and small HoReCa. They run SAR 50k–500k monthly working capital, buy from several FMCG suppliers, operate largely in cash, and have little or no access to formal credit because they lack the documents and collateral banks require.

Why banks can't

No invoices, no statements, no collateral, no formal credit history. A bank cannot underwrite a shop that keeps its books in its owner's head — and cannot collect from one without a field presence it will never build. The segment is structurally abandoned, which is the opportunity and the trap.

The diagnosis

Three facts that decide everything

Fact 01 · the killer

Repayment, not approval

In a cash economy the loan is easy to give and hard to get back. Today's 95% on-time settlement is real — but it is earned by high-touch, manual follow-up that does not scale to tens of thousands of shops. Loss is driven by collection failure, not by saying "yes" too often. The only proven way to lend here at scale — Square, Tienda Pago, MaxAB — is to repay from the payment flow itself, not to chase it afterward.

Fact 02 · the clock

The corner is crowding

Erad already runs live embedded credit and is moving into distribution. Sary already runs POS BNPL with its own baqala salesforce. Tamara has a B2B product and a $2.4B facility. The "Leaders" corner is filling before Tradepay arrives. We are last to market.

Fact 03 · the asymmetry

We are weak where they're strong

Rivals lead on capital (Tamara, Lendo), live product (Erad, Sary), and distribution (Sary). Our single structural advantage is collections-at-source — and it is the one thing not yet proven. The strategy must serve proving that one number before anything else.

The bad-strategy trap we refuse. The easy answer is "build a great app and acquire a million merchants." That mistakes a goal for a strategy. It ignores the binding constraint (collection), picks a fight on the axis where we're weakest (acquisition spend), and assumes growth is the same as a healthy book. In a cash economy, blitzscaling a loan book is how it dies — losses lag growth by 6–12 months, so the dashboard lies until the book seasons.
So the challenge, stated plainly: become the one player whose repayment is captured structurally rather than chased — fast enough, and cheaply enough, to be lower-loss than every rival before they out-capitalise us. Everything that follows is in service of that, or it is fluff.

Chapter 03
Kernel · Guiding policy

Own the rail. Everything else rides on it.

The guiding policy is a single, focused choice that channels action: capture repayment at the source of the merchant's cash flow. Credit rides the payment rail; the loan is repaid before the merchant can divert the money. This is the defensible niche — not a feature, a structural position.

Wedge

The Rail

A closed-loop settlement + auto-debit layer. Repayment is taken at source via SARIE and rep cash-in — so we know within seconds, not 30 days, whether a payment landed. This is the one thing structurally solving collections.

Brain

The Engine

Two-layer decisioning (pre-score + real-time) that approves more of a distributor's merchants at the same loss. The commercial argument that opens every channel: "+15pp on your book at your loss rate — audited, or you don't pay."

Endgame

The Arm

White-label the proven stack so distributors run their own financing on Tradepay — converting the named competitive threat (in-house arms) into the customer base and the TAM.

The MOAT · why this compounds and can't be copied

Four moats that deepen with every cohort

1 · Collections moat

Auto-debit at source means lower DPD, lower loss, cheaper credit, which wins the distributor's next deal. A rival chasing repayment after the fact cannot match the loss curve — and loss is the whole game.

2 · Data moat

Every transaction is a labelled outcome (repaid / defaulted). A two-year-old engine beats a zero-year-old one by multiples on the hard 40th-percentile merchants. Time-based; uncompressible.

3 · Distribution moat

The distributor's existing reps activate credit at near-zero marginal cost. Switching means retraining the field force and losing the data edge we earned them. The rep network is a channel no pure-play can build.

4 · Capital moat

A seasoned tape funds at warehouse-then-securitisation rates a new entrant can't touch. A 300+bps funding-cost edge on a growing book compounds into pricing power.

Differentiation · the collateral question, answered honestly

Flow-secured at the shop, asset-secured at the warehouse

Shop stock is bad collateral: it is self-liquidating (sold to repay — that's the model), commingled, low-value, and KSA's movable-pledge law lets the merchant sell it in the ordinary course anyway. Seizing a leased baqala and re-selling it turns a credit rail into a distressed-SME operator — the opposite of lean. So the collateral that's actually worth having is the future flow (captured by the rail) plus the distributor's supply-cutoff lever ("no repay, no stock") and bureau reporting. Real asset-collateral only earns its keep at the larger wholesale tier — a registered floating charge over receivables, the Erad/Lendo pattern. Tradepay is the only player designing both origination tiers under one funding structure.

Defensible vs each rival type. Vs traditional lenders — we collect where they can't reach. Vs fintech startups — we own a rail and a channel, not just an app. Vs distributor in-house arms — they can extend relationship credit but can't underwrite or fund at scale, so we white-label them. The moat is the composition, not any single layer.

Chapter 07
Coherent action · the lever

The whole strategy rests on one person.

Tradepay never hires a merchant salesforce. The distributor's existing route rep is the channel — already trusted, already visiting weekly. The model lives or dies on whether he activates credit. So we design for him, not around him.

Persona

Faisal — the route rep

Drives a stocked van down a fixed beat, visits 20–30 baqalas a day, knows every owner by name, and already carries an SFA app (FieldAssist / Bizom). He isn't selling credit — he's selling more Pepsi. Credit is just the tool that lets the shop buy more this week. He's paid on volume he moves and trusts what he can explain in one line.

The risk we design against

The agent moral-hazard trap

Paying reps per activation is exactly the incentive that has burned agent-network lenders worldwide — reps push loans onto bad borrowers to earn the bonus, and adverse selection poisons the book. So the bonus must vest on repayment, with clawback on early default. This isn't a detail; it's the difference between a healthy book and a dead one.

The pattern is proven — it just lives in a different stack. FieldAssist already ties FMCG rep incentives to collections, not just orders, with leaderboards and real-time payout tracking; Bizom runs the tiered discount-calculator pattern. None of the named credit rivals build for the rep. The whitespace is real, the mechanics are validated, and the right move is to embed inside the rep's existing SFA app — not to ship a rival app.

Chapter 08
Coherent action · source of advantage

The engine is the argument. Risk is the discipline.

The decision engine is what lets us approve more of a distributor's merchants at the same loss — the line that opens every channel. But the engine only earns trust if risk is modelled with the rigour of a regulated lender. Both, or neither.

Layer 1 · pre-score

Cold-start from partner data

Before a merchant ever applies, we pre-score from distributor history — purchase frequency, invoice values, SKU diversity, supplier relationships — and assign a risk tier and revolving limit. The merchant is approved at the counter because the work happened beforehand.

Layer 2 · real-time

Transaction-level decisioning

At order time the engine approves, declines, or partially finances in under a second, checking the transaction against the merchant's exposure, velocity, and risk profile. SKU-level dynamic limits; partial approvals instead of hard noes.

Risk modelling · how we actually build the models

A regulated-lender stack, not a startup score

Provisioning

IFRS 9 / ECL

Expected-credit-loss provisioning with 3-stage staging from origination. PD × LGD × EAD, forward-looking macro overlays (Ramadan/Hajj seasonality, oil-cycle). The provisioning the warehouse and auditors require.

Monitoring

DPD buckets & roll rates

Days-past-due buckets with roll-rate transition matrices — the early-warning system. Same-day knowledge from the rail means roll rates are observed in real time, not reconstructed monthly.

Validation

Vintage & champion/challenger

Cohort vintage curves prove (or disprove) the "losses fall as cohorts season" thesis. New models run as challengers against the champion on live traffic before promotion.

Explainability

Decisions a merchant, partner, and regulator can read

Every decline returns a plain-language adverse-action reason ("limit reached this cycle," not "model said no"). Reason codes are logged, auditable, and surfaced in the merchant app and partner console. Model governance keeps the engine examinable — a SAMA and AECB requirement, and a trust feature.

Feedback loops

Every repayment improves the next decision

Repayment outcomes, merchant feedback, and portfolio performance flow back as labelled training data nightly. The data flywheel is the moat: a seasoned engine beats a cold one by multiples on the hard 40th-percentile merchant.

Fraud & financial crime · product-level safeguards
Document fraud

AI-forged bank statements are now a live threat (the CBA case). Upload-path safeguards: liveness, source-verification, and tamper detection — friction only where risk spikes, never blanket.

AML / monitoring

Transaction-monitoring scenario library run with the lender-of-record bank; velocity and structuring checks on the rail. Nafath identity anchors KYB.

Collusion

Rep-merchant collusion is the channel's native risk; repayment-vested bonuses + geo-tagged activation + cohort anomaly detection contain it.

Data & insights strategy

From transactions to value — and a data-informed culture

Product analytics

North-star: repaying active merchants. KPIs: approval rate at constant loss, on-rail repayment %, DPD roll rates, attach (avg outstanding), cohort retention, revenue/FTE.

Merchant insights

Cash-flow view, reorder timing, SKU-mix benchmarks vs similar shops — turning their own data into better purchasing.

Partner insights

Settlement reporting, merchant-health scores, credit-uplift dashboards — the stickiness layer for distributors.

Data culture

Weekly metric review, every PRD ships with its measurement plan, decisions cite the tape. Receipts over claims.

The three winners aren't rivals — they compose across time: Rail (wedge) → Engine (advantage) → Arm (endgame). That sequencing is what makes the answer feel inevitable rather than arbitrary.

Risks we own
Loss-rate overshoot

If losses sit at 5%+ instead of trending to 2.5%, EBITDA compresses hard. Mitigation: the 12-month pilot, vintage caps, and risk brake authority. Lendo's real 4.5% is the benchmark we model against, not 2.5%.

The anchor race

If Erad or Sary lock the best distributors first, we lose the channels. Mitigation: the CEO closes the first two anchors personally with the measured test; founder credibility, not spend.

Funding freeze

If Islamic capital markets tighten, cost of funds jumps. Mitigation: warehouse extends; securitisation defers a year; growth gates to capital availability.

Dashboards · the same truth, four lenses

Each audience sees the metric that drives their decision

Board / exec

North-star, EBITDA path, book size, loss vs budget, cost of funds, revenue per human. One screen, monthly.

Distributor (partner)

Credit-uplift on their book, settlement timeliness, merchant-health scores, and the rep activation leaderboard. The stickiness layer.

Merchant

Limit, utilization, upcoming due dates, on-time streak, and the progress bar to the next limit raise. Trust through transparency.

Risk console

DPD buckets, vintage curves, roll-rate matrices, circuit-breaker status, and model-drift alerts — observed live off the rail.

Data-informed culture

Every PRD ships with its measurement plan; the weekly review opens on the north-star and the loss curve; decisions cite the tape, not opinion. Agents generate the dashboards; humans interrogate them.

Anti-vanity stance

GMV and merchant count are reported as diagnostics, never as success. Two numbers sit on the wall: repaying flow on the rail, and loss vs budget. If a metric can rise while the book quietly rots, it doesn't get to define winning.
