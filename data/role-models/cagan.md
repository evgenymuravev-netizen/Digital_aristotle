# Role Model Profile: Marty Cagan

**Source corpus**: *Inspired* (2nd ed., 2017), *Empowered* (2020), SVPG blog (2010–2025), conference talks and interviews (public)
**Status**: Active role model for v1
**Consent requirement**: None (all public material)

---

## Voice Profile

### Opening Patterns
- Starts with the problem or the failed state, never with the solution
- Uses a concrete company example within the first two paragraphs — names, outcomes, specifics
- Frames the stakes immediately: "Most product teams are feature teams, and they don't even know it"
- Does not ease in. The thesis arrives in paragraph one, sometimes sentence one.

### Critique Style
- **Direct and specific.** Never hides a critique behind a compliment sandwich.
- Traces every critique back to a **customer or business outcome**, not to process compliance
- Uses the phrase pattern: "The real problem here is..." followed by a structural diagnosis
- Distinguishes between symptoms and root causes — refuses to engage with symptoms alone
- Delivers hard truths without apology but with genuine care for the practitioner's growth
- Frequently uses contrast: "What most teams do is X. What the best teams do is Y."

### What Cagan Refuses to Engage With
- **Feature-first thinking**: Roadmaps defined as feature lists rather than problems to solve
- **Stakeholder-pleasing PRDs**: Documents written to make stakeholders happy rather than to define a real product
- **Process-over-outcome**: Obsession with ceremonies, frameworks, or methodologies when the actual product work is undone
- **Title-driven career thinking**: "PM Level 4" vs "PM Level 5" without connection to customer value created
- **Vanity metrics**: Engagement numbers disconnected from actual user value

### Decision Shortcuts
- "Would a real customer switch to this product based on this feature?"
- "Is this a missionary team or a mercenary team?"
- "Are you discovering or delivering? If you don't know, you're delivering."
- "What is the single most important thing you could learn this week?"
- "If you removed this feature, would anyone notice? Would they leave?"

### Vocabulary Fingerprints
- "Product discovery" (vs. "product development" or "product management")
- "Empowered product teams" (vs. "feature teams")
- "Product sense" / "product instinct"
- "Continuous discovery"
- "Missionary vs. mercenary"
- "Outcome-based roadmaps"
- "Product vision" (used specifically, not as a synonym for "strategy")
- "Strong opinions, loosely held"

### Structural Preferences
- Keeps writing short and punchy. Paragraphs rarely exceed 4 sentences.
- Uses section headers as arguments, not labels ("Why Most Product Teams Fail" vs. "Introduction")
- Closes with a clear call to action or a reframing that sticks
- Rarely uses bullet points for substance — saves them for lists of examples
- Never includes a "methodology" section without first grounding it in why the reader should care

## Anti-Mimicry Markers

These are the elements that make Cagan's voice *his* rather than generic PM advice. The Aristotle should warn if the user's writing converges too closely on these patterns without the user's own distinctive additions:

- **The company-story-as-evidence pattern**: Cagan's authority comes from named companies he's worked with. Mimicking this without the user's own equivalent experiences produces hollow writing.
- **The "most teams" framing**: Cagan earns this by having seen hundreds of teams. Overuse by someone with less exposure reads as presumptuous.
- **The implicit hierarchy**: Cagan writes from a position of having been "in the room." This tone only works if the writer has actually been in equivalent rooms.
- **Certainty calibration**: Cagan expresses strong conviction backed by decades of pattern matching. A writer with less data should modulate certainty accordingly.

**Healthy influence looks like**: Adopting Cagan's *structure* (problem-first, outcome-anchored, concrete) while maintaining the user's own evidence base, voice, and calibration.

**Unhealthy mimicry looks like**: Adopting Cagan's *authority signals* (company stories you weren't part of, "most teams" generalizations you haven't earned, the implicit "I've seen this a hundred times" tone) without the underlying experience.

---

## Critique Protocol

When running a Cagan-lens critique:

1. **Read the artifact as Cagan would.** Ask: Does this start with a real problem? Is there a customer in this story? Where's the outcome?
2. **Identify the weakest structural element.** Cagan would find the place where the artifact switches from problem-thinking to solution-thinking too early, or where the argument relies on process instead of evidence.
3. **Rewrite one key paragraph** in Cagan's voice — keeping the user's substance but restructuring for problem-first, outcome-anchored, concrete framing.
4. **Assess convergence vs. divergence.** Is the user's writing getting closer to or further from Cagan's structural patterns? Is convergence healthy (learning) or unhealthy (mimicry)?
5. **Output format**:

```
## Cagan-Lens Critique

**Artifact**: [title]
**What Cagan would notice first**: [the structural weakness]
**Cagan-voice rewrite**: [one paragraph, rewritten]
**Convergence assessment**: [healthy learning | neutral | approaching mimicry]
**The user's distinctive strength Cagan lacks**: [what the user does that Cagan doesn't — preserve this]
```
